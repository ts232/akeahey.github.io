Which Translation of Les Miserables Is The Best?

As a long time lover of Les Miserables, a discussion with a friend about the thematic differences between the English and French versions of the novel got me thinking: could the translation of the novel from French to English has altered it's emotional meaning? Are some translations better than others at staying true to Hugo's original emotional intent?

In order to explore this question, I ran two English translations of Les Mis through sentiment analysis in R and compared them to the results of the same process on the original French.

Below is a side by side comparison of the basic positive/negative sentiment of the two English translations, using the same nrc database for sentiment. Clearly, these graphs show that a difference exists between the two translations in terms of overall sentiment.

A Personal Note:

After this data exploration, I also offer my own, subjective measure of a translation's value.

In chapter VI of Book I of Volume IV, Enjolras says "be serious," a line which is translated the same way in every translation from the original "sois serieux".

However, in the next line, Grantaire responds "je suis farouche," which has been translated many different ways, including "timid" (Wraxal, 1887), "wild" (Hapgood, 1862), and "unflinching" (Denny, 1976).

The meaning of the French word farouche lies somewhere between the English "feral" and "shy"- like a cornered, untamed animal that recoils from being touched.

Given this definition and the context in which it is said, Hapgood's translation to "wild" seems to be the most accurate.

While one word is not enough to write a translation's sentence, this particular line is beloved by many fans of Les Miserables, including myself, and gives it a special commemoration in the eyes of dedicated readers.
